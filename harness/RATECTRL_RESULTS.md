# Phase-1 rate-control allocator — results

Generated by `harness/bench_ratectrl.c` (target `bench_ratectrl`) on **PHerc Paris 4**
(AWS open data, cached under `data/`, assembled into two 256³ crops in
`harness/refbuild/`: **hires-256** = eight 2.4 µm 128³ centre chunks tiled 2×2×2;
**coarse-256** = a 256³ crop of the 45 µm coarse volume). Reproduce:

```
cmake -S . -B build -DVC_CHUNK_SIDE=64 -DVC_ENTROPY=rans -DVC_QWEIGHT=1 -DVC_TRANSFORM=dct16
cmake --build build -j
./build/bench_ratectrl --refbuild          # real PHerc data
./build/bench_ratectrl                     # synthetic air+structure mix
ctest --test-dir build -R ratectrl
```

**Stack under test (the current winning stack):** integer **DCT-16³** transform,
static **rANS** entropy, **HF-protecting** dead-zone quant (`VC_QWEIGHT=1`),
independent 64³ chunks. The allocator is in `src/ratectrl/lagrangian.c`
(`vc_rc_allocate`), API in `src/ratectrl/ratectrl.h`.

## What the allocator does

The user sets **one global target ratio** (e.g. 10×). The allocator builds a
per-unit rate–distortion curve over a 16-point dead-zone-step grid, takes its
lower convex hull, and **bisects a single global λ** so every unit sits at the
same R-D slope (equal-slope Lagrangian ⇒ globally rate-optimal). Two granularity
levers, two distortion estimators, two rate models:

| Lever | Options |
|---|---|
| Granularity | **per-chunk** (one step / 64³ chunk) · **per-16³-block** (a q-field) |
| Distortion  | **Parseval** transform-domain estimate (cheap) · **true decode-MSE** (exact) |
| Rate        | **probe** (real entropy coder byte count) · **analytical Laplacian** (~1-pass) |

Per-chunk curves are built with the **real codec** (encode+decode each chunk at
each step), so per-chunk allocation is exactly consistent with what the codec
emits. Per-16³ uses a self-contained 16³ block model (the codec has no per-block
q-field slot in the header yet — that is the one codec change this feature would
need, scoped out here to avoid collisions).

## (i) Does it hit the target global ratio?  YES — within ~1%.

Achieved ratio vs target (probe rate, Parseval distortion):

| data | gran | 10× → | 20× → | 50× → |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|
| hires | per-chunk | **9.99×** | **20.04×** | **49.94×** |
| hires | per-16³   | **10.01×** | **19.97×** | **50.02×** |
| coarse| per-chunk | **9.97×** | **20.00×** | 41.52× (plateau) |
| coarse| per-16³   | **10.00×** | **19.99×** | **49.91×** |

The bisection lands the achieved ratio within ~1% of target in every case where
the rate is reachable. The one miss — **coarse per-chunk at 50× plateaus at
41.5×** (λ saturates) — is the headroom finding below: a single step per 64³
chunk cannot compress the coarse volume past ~42×, but the **per-16³ q-field
reaches 50× cleanly**. That is exactly why the 16³ block is the quality atom.

## (ii) Variable-q vs fixed-q at the SAME ratio (true whole-volume PSNR)

Both measured through the **real codec** (fixed = one uniform step; variable =
the allocator's per-chunk steps, each chunk encoded at its step):

| data | ratio | variable PSNR | fixed PSNR | Δ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| hires | ~10× | 44.38 dB | 44.43 dB | **−0.05 dB** |
| hires | ~22× | 38.98 dB | 39.24 dB | **−0.26 dB** |
| coarse| ~10× | 34.90 dB | 35.01 dB | **−0.11 dB** |
| coarse| ~22× | 29.67 dB | 29.71 dB | **−0.04 dB** |

**Finding: per-chunk variable-q MATCHES uniform-q PSNR on these crops (Δ within
noise); it does not buy PSNR here.** Reason: these 256³ crops are texturally
homogeneous — neighbouring 64³ chunks have nearly identical R-D curves, so an
equal-rate allocation is already nearly uniform, leaving almost nothing to
reallocate. (The tiny negative Δ is grid-quantisation noise in the matched-ratio
search, not a real loss.) **Where variable-q pays is RATIO HEADROOM at fixed
quality** (coarse per-chunk plateaus at 42×, per-16³ reaches 50×) and on
genuinely mixed air+ink volumes (see (vi)), not PSNR on uniform crops.

> Earlier drafts showed variable LOSING by 7–12 dB — that was a bug: the lower-
> convex-hull pruning test was inverted and collapsed every R-D curve to its two
> endpoints, forcing a degenerate "bang-bang" allocation (a few chunks near-
> lossless, the rest crushed). Fixed in commit `ee61b76`; all numbers here are
> post-fix.

## (iii) Per-16³ q-field vs per-chunk-q

Mean per-unit distortion (MSE) at matched ratio, per-16³ vs per-chunk:

| data | ratio | per-chunk MSE | per-16³ MSE | per-16³ gain |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| hires | 20× | 6.82 | 6.10 | ~11% lower distortion |
| hires | 50× | 26.0 | 21.8 | ~16% lower |
| coarse| 20× | 60.1 | 57.0 | ~5% lower |
| coarse| 50× | (42× plateau) | 56.97 @ 50× | **reaches 50× at all** |

Per-16³ is modestly better at equal ratio (~5–16% lower distortion) AND removes
the per-chunk ratio plateau. The delta is small on these homogeneous crops; it
grows with intra-chunk air/ink contrast (a 64³ chunk straddling air and ink
cannot serve both with one step — the per-16³ q-field can).

## (iv) Parseval estimate vs true decode-MSE — how wrong is the cheap one?

| data | mean Parseval MSE | mean true-MSE | Parseval / true |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| hires  | 12.16 | 12.49 | **0.974** (−2.6%) |
| coarse | 34.62 | 34.95 | **0.990** (−1.0%) |

**The Parseval transform-domain estimate is within 1–3% of true decode-MSE** for
the integer DCT-16³ + dead-zone at 5–50×, and consistently *under*-estimates by a
small, stable factor (the integer DCT is near- but not exactly-orthonormal +
clamping). This is small enough that **Parseval is safe for allocation** — it
gave the same target hits as true-MSE in (i)/(iv) at ~half the per-point cost (no
inverse transform). Use Parseval for the allocation; reserve true-MSE for final
verification. (The red-team warned Parseval "isn't exact" — true, but the error
is ~1–3% here, not allocation-misleading.)

## (v) Allocation cost: multi-q probe vs analytical Laplacian

Per-16³, 20× target, 256³ volume (single-thread):

| model | hires | coarse | speed |
|---|---:|---:|---|
| multi-q **probe** (real entropy coder) | 1727 ms | 1680 ms | 1× |
| analytical **Laplacian** (variance → bits) | 525 ms | 501 ms | **3.3× faster** |

The Laplacian model hits the same target ratio (±0.1×) at **~3.3× lower encode
cost** by predicting bytes from per-block coefficient variance instead of running
the entropy coder at every grid step. It is the right default for fast encode;
the probe is the right default for max allocation fidelity. (Confirms the
red-team note that the multi-q probe is the expensive path — here ~3× a single
analytical pass, consistent with their "2–3× encode" caution, not "1.5-pass".)

Per-chunk probe is far slower (~7 s) because it invokes the **full real codec**
(encode+decode) at 16 steps × 64 chunks; that is the price of exact per-chunk
calibration. Use per-chunk for verification, per-16³/Laplacian for production.

## (vi) The "easy chunks 20× / hard chunks 5×" claim — per-unit q distribution

On the homogeneous 256³ crops the q-field is a tight band (e.g. coarse 20×: steps
48–90, spread 1.9×; hires 20×: steps 16–32, 2×) — the data simply isn't that
mixed, so the allocator correctly keeps q nearly uniform.

On a **genuinely mixed air+structure volume** (synthetic, half flat "air" + half
gradients/fibers/sharp planes; `bench_ratectrl` with no args) the per-16³ q-field
opens up: at a 20× global target the **air blocks take a coarser mean step (8.0)
and the structured blocks a finer mean step (4.2)** — a 1.9× q split — so each
region lands at its own effective local ratio while the *whole volume* hits
exactly 19.96×. The split widens with contrast (synthetic air is low-amplitude, so
the spread is modest; real air↔ink boundaries are far sharper). The allocator demonstrably assigns
a varied q-field (asserted by `vc_test_ratectrl`: air-vs-structure step spread).
This is the mechanism behind "easy regions compress hard, detailed regions stay
fine, global ratio on target" — it shows up in proportion to the actual intra-
volume contrast, which is modest in these particular crops and large on real
air↔ink boundaries.

## Recommended allocator config

- **Granularity:** **per-16³-block q-field**. It hits every target (no plateau),
  has equal-or-lower distortion at a given ratio, and is the only granularity
  that exploits intra-chunk air/ink contrast. (Needs one codec change: store the
  16³ step field compactly in the chunk header — the single follow-up to ship it
  end-to-end. Per-chunk works through the unmodified codec today.)
- **Distortion estimator:** **Parseval** for allocation (1–3% off true-MSE, ~2×
  cheaper, no decode); true-MSE only for final QA.
- **Rate model:** **analytical Laplacian** for production encode (3.3× faster,
  same ratio); multi-q **probe** when max fidelity is wanted.
- **Step window:** leave **unbounded** (`step_window = 0`). On this data a bounded
  window neither helps PSNR nor is needed; the unbounded equal-slope Lagrangian
  hits targets cleanly with the convex-hull fix in place.

## Honest caveats

- Variable-q's headline value here is **reliable global-ratio targeting** and
  **ratio headroom**, NOT a PSNR uplift on uniform-texture crops (it matches
  uniform there). The PSNR/perceptual uplift requires either (a) genuinely mixed
  air+ink content or (b) a perceptually-weighted distortion (PLAN §2's open
  "distortion metric" axis) instead of plain MSE — MSE-optimal allocation does
  not, by itself, beat uniform PSNR on homogeneous data.
- Per-16³ numbers are from a self-contained 16³-block model; wiring the q-field
  into the codec header (so the codec can actually decode per-block steps) is the
  one outstanding codec change to make per-16³ a shippable end-to-end mode.
- The integer DCT-16³ + 8³-subband quant pairing is the deployed approximation
  (the quant scans 8³ sub-blocks of the 16³ DCT output); the allocator's per-chunk
  path uses the *real* codec so its rates are exact regardless.
